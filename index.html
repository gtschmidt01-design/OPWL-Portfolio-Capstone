Evaluation

United States Coast Guard Emergency Medical Technician Recertification Process Evaluation — conducted by Brian Christina, Blake George, & Gregory Schmidt

Background

Organization

The United States Coast Guard (USCG) is a military service and maritime law enforcement agency within the Department of Homeland Security. Its missions span search and rescue, maritime law enforcement, environmental response, ports and waterways security, and defense operations.

Because the USCG fields a relatively small active-duty force, members are frequently assigned to geographically dispersed units with limited access to centralized training facilities — a constraint that shapes how training programs like EMT recertification have to be delivered.

Program and Stakeholders

USCG members routinely encounter medical emergencies in remote or operationally demanding environments, so the organization maintains a credentialed EMT workforce. Emergency medical capability is a core readiness requirement for air station rescue swimmers, boat crew members, clinics, and many afloat personnel — often under conditions that differ substantially from civilian Emergency Medical Services (EMS) settings, including offshore rescues, dive operations, and multi-casualty incidents far from definitive care.

Beyond the CEU requirement, each cycle requires in-person psychomotor skills verification, coordinated locally through unit medical staff or Local EMT Medical Advisors (LEMAs) — a relatively new role, with some advisors reporting limited familiarity with oversight and recertification processes. Completed documentation is submitted to Training Center (TRACEN) Petaluma, or to Health, Safety, and Work-Life (HSWL) for Health Service Technicians.

Evaluation Request

During the Spring 2026 semester, our team evaluated the USCG's EMT recertification process at the request of Force Readiness Command (FORCECOM) and TRACEN Petaluma leadership. The goal: determine whether the current model supports consistent skills verification and sustained skill retention across the certification cycle, and inform decisions about future program improvements.

The current distributed model — built around online continuing education and locally coordinated skills verification — replaced a centralized resident course at TRACEN Petaluma that was discontinued during the pandemic and never reinstated. A 2020 cost-benefit analysis supported the shift on efficiency grounds (USCG, FORCECOM, TRACEN Petaluma, 2020), and a follow-up analysis in November 2025 further examined delivery options (USCG, FORCECOM, TRACEN Petaluma, 2025). While more flexible and cost-efficient, the distributed model raised fleet-wide concerns: inconsistent skills-verification practices, possible skill degradation, and uneven evaluator access — particularly for remote or small units that sometimes require significant travel to reach a qualified evaluator. These concerns, along with open questions about documentation standards and verifier preparation, prompted the evaluation.

Evaluation Methods

My team followed a formative, back-end, goal-based evaluation using Chyung's (2019) 10-step evaluation procedure as the primary framework, paired with the Training Impact Model (TIM) to organize the evaluand.

Why Chyung's 10-Step Model?

Chyung's (2019) framework was selected because it is purpose-built for evaluating training and performance improvement programs in organizational settings — the exact context of the USCG EMT recertification program. Unlike general program evaluation models (e.g., CIPP or Logic Model approaches), Chyung's model integrates importance weighting into the evaluative process, which was essential for our work because FORCECOM and TRACEN Petaluma leadership needed findings that were explicitly tied to what mattered most for readiness decisions. The model also calls for multiple data sources and formal evaluative conclusions rather than descriptive summaries, which increased the credibility and actionability of our report.

Why TIM?

TIM was used alongside Chyung's framework to ensure we treated the recertification program as a performance system — with interconnected inputs, processes, and outputs — rather than evaluating it as a single training course. This distinction mattered because the program involves multiple actors (LEMAs, unit medical staff, TRACEN Petaluma, HSWL), distributed execution across geographically dispersed units, and outcomes tied directly to fleet readiness. TIM helped us map those relationships and avoid artificially narrowing the scope to just the CEU completion rate.

Dimensions and Importance Weighting

Dimensions were determined through a structured review process focused on stakeholder needs, TIM, USCG EMS policy, NREMT recertification requirements, and the intended use of findings by FORCECOM and TRACEN Petaluma leadership. Both dimensions were confirmed with sponsors before data collection began, and importance weighting was set by those stakeholders based on their readiness priorities.

Data Collection

The evaluation team used an extant records review, a policy and guidance review, an anonymous survey, and semi-structured interviews as the primary sources for the data review. These sources directly complement the two dimensions of Skill Verification Consistency and Program Capability for Skill Retention. The anonymous survey provided some of the most valuable data, as no prior survey information on the program previously existed.

  • Extant records review — existing documentation of skills verification submissions and program records.
  • Policy and guidance review — NREMT and USCG EMS policy expectations for verifier authorization and documentation.
  • Anonymous survey — fleet-wide input on readiness, skill retention, and field experience with the verification process.
  • Semi-structured interviews — background context and program orientation from program stakeholders.

Results

How We Reached Our Conclusions

Evaluative conclusions were reached by applying a performance rubric to each dimension, drawing on all four data sources. Survey items were analyzed quantitatively — frequency distributions and descriptive statistics were used to identify patterns across respondents — and open-ended responses were coded thematically. Policy and extant records were reviewed against documented NREMT and USCG EMS standards and scored against pre-established performance indicators. Each dimension was then assigned an evaluative rating (Exemplary, Good, Mediocre, or Poor) based on the degree to which the collected evidence met the performance standards set in Step 5 of Chyung's framework. Importance weights set by the client guided how much each dimension influenced the overall program judgment.

Reflections

The evaluation process provided a valuable real-world application of program evaluation. Working with an actual evaluand and client allowed me to experience the full 10-step evaluation framework in practice, rather than just in theory. Seeing how each step built upon the previous one reinforced the importance of a structured and systematic approach. One of the most significant takeaways was the role of stakeholder engagement throughout the process — actively involving stakeholders not only informed decision-making but also ensured the evaluation remained relevant and useful to the client's needs. Given the project's limited timeframe, addressing scope early on was essential; initial client meetings highlighted the risks of scope creep, making it necessary to clearly define priorities and focus on the most critical dimensions. This narrowing and refining of scope was a key skill that strengthened the overall effectiveness of the project. Overall, this experience established a strong foundation for future evaluation work, increased my confidence in applying the 10-step framework, and highlighted the importance of balancing structure, stakeholder input, and practical constraints in real-world settings.

Instructional Design

Emergency Drill Debriefs — Idaho Youth Ranch, an Instructional Design project completed by Brian Christina, Blake George, Gregory Schmidt, and Brandy Young

Challenge

Program

Idaho Youth Ranch (IYR) is an Idaho-based non-profit that offers support to local youth, including emergency shelter, residential care, therapy, life coaching, and similar services. Founded in 1953, IYR's values of relationships, growth, commitment, transparency, compassion, and joy continue to shape how it functions. IYR operates three separate sites run by program managers and staffed by a variety of workers supporting youth. Because all three sites involve some aspect of residential care, emergency drills — ranging from routine fire drills to active-shooter scenarios — are conducted regularly. While part of required Joint Commission accreditation, these drills serve as an intentional avenue for ensuring the current and future safety of staff and youth.

Design Request

In fulfilling the Joint Commission requirement for emergency drills, debriefs are also required following each drill. The central stakeholders — IYR's Health and Safety Officer and on-site program managers — identified the need for a more structured, consistent, and impactful debrief process. Though debriefs were expected, the debrief structure was not standardized, leading to a missed opportunity for strengthening IYR's response readiness. Program managers lacked guidance on what a debrief should include, how it should be facilitated, who should lead it, and how outcomes should be documented. As a result, each site approached debriefs differently, limiting the organization's ability to learn systematically from drills and real incidents — a missed opportunity both for ensuring learning in high-stress environments and for capturing valuable lessons learned.

Goals

The primary goal of this project was to design a practical, field-ready toolkit that would equip IYR program managers to facilitate consistent, high-quality emergency drill debriefs across all three residential sites. Specifically, the project aimed to:

  • Standardize the debrief process — provide a shared structure that all sites could follow, reducing the current variability in how debriefs were conducted and documented.
  • Build facilitator capacity — create a resource that supported managers at every experience level, from those with no prior debrief experience to those already using informal approaches, without requiring significant preparation time.
  • Improve learning from drills — shift debriefs from a compliance checkbox into a genuine learning opportunity that captures lessons learned and informs future emergency preparedness.
  • Support Joint Commission documentation requirements — ensure that the process and its outputs aligned with IYR's accreditation obligations, reducing risk and supporting organizational accountability.
  • Design for real-world constraints — produce a tool that was immediately usable in high-stress, time-limited environments without requiring extensive training or setup.

Approach

We approached this project through a Training Requirements Analysis (TRA), Learner and Environmental Analysis (LEA), and Task Analysis (TA) using interviews, a focus group, and existing organizational data. A key insight during the analysis phase was recognizing that, though accreditation requirements were a clear driver, leadership consistently emphasized something deeper: building staff capability, not just checking a compliance box. This realization shifted our approach from designing for standardization to designing for capacity building.

From the TRA, LEA, and TA, several key patterns emerged:

  • Facilitator experience varied widely — some managers had never led a formal debrief, while others relied on informal approaches, and still others were well-versed from past career experience, such as the military.
  • Time pressure was constant — staff work in demanding residential care environments with competing responsibilities; any tool requiring significant time investment was unlikely to be used consistently.
  • Existing practices had value — some managers were already doing parts of debriefing well, even without a formal structure.
  • Debriefs were missing potential — though conducted, the opportunity to increase staff capacity and effectiveness for real emergencies was being missed.

Decisions and Mindset

The design of the facilitator guide and job aid was driven by a series of deliberate decisions grounded in theory, analysis, and real-world constraints.

Theoretical Foundations in Practice

Each theory and model was chosen deliberately based on what the analyses revealed about the learners, the environment, and the performance gap. The questions below shaped how each was applied: What was the purpose of this model? What did it tell us? How did it drive a design decision?

Cognitive Load Theory SWELLER

Purpose: Explains how the brain manages limited working memory — chunking and simplifying information reduces cognitive strain.
What it told us: The debrief happens immediately after a high-stress drill; facilitators cannot manage a complex, multi-page guide in that moment.
Design decision: All content was consolidated onto a single page with clear, chunked steps and minimal extraneous detail.

Adult Learning Theory KNOWLES

Purpose: Adults learn best when the content is experience-driven, practical, and relevant to their real lives.
What it told us: IYR staff had existing debrief experience — some formal, some informal — and resisted being told how to "start from scratch."
Design decision: Reflection prompts were built around lived debrief scenarios, and the tool was framed as enhancing what staff already do well.

Situated Learning Theory LAVE & WENGER

Purpose: Learning and performance are most effective when they occur in the context where the skill is actually applied.
What it told us: Training managers on debrief facilitation in a classroom or asynchronous setting would not transfer — the skill needed to be supported in the room, during the debrief itself.
Design decision: The job aid was designed as an in-context tool, not a pre-study reference.

Behavior Engineering Model GILBERT

Purpose: Distinguishes between environmental and individual factors causing a performance gap — and pushes toward environmental solutions first.
What it told us: The gap was not in individual knowledge or motivation; it was in the absence of clear information (no shared structure) and resources (no usable tool) — see Figure 1 below.
Design decision: The deliverable became a job aid rather than a training course.

The analysis pointed to a gap in the environment — facilitators lacked a shared structure and a tool they could use in real time — rather than a gap in individual knowledge or motivation. That distinction is why the deliverable became a job aid, not a training course.

Solutions

Designing for Use in the Moment

We had to decide whether to create a comprehensive facilitator guide or a tool that could function in real time. Given the time pressure managers described, we prioritized a one-page job aid usable immediately during a debrief with minimal preparation, aligning the tool with how work actually happens, including real-time emergencies.

Designing for First-Time Facilitators

Because facilitator experience ranged from none to informal, we designed the tool as a "zero-prep" guide. Each step not only directs action but also explains its purpose, allowing facilitators to build confidence while using it.

Preserving and Strengthening Existing Practices

Rather than imposing a rigid process, we introduced a consistent structure that supports what managers already do well, increasing the tool's intuitiveness and adaptability across all three sites.

Designing for Cognitive Load

Given the high-stress context of emergency drills, we reduced cognitive demand by consolidating prompts, structure, and documentation into a single page, minimizing the need for facilitators to recall steps or navigate multiple resources in real time.

Accessibility

The tool was designed to be usable across a wide range of facilitators and contexts. Font size, visual hierarchy, clear headings, jargon-free language, and logical flow all ensure that the tools are usable by managers with varying educational backgrounds, neurodiverse needs, under time pressure, and in high-stress environments. Design choices were guided by the Contrast, Alignment, Repetition, and Proximity (CARP) model to ensure that every visual and layout decision reinforces clarity, reduces cognitive friction, and supports consistent use across IYR's three sites.

Constraints

  • Limited time for preparation
  • Inconsistent facilitation experience
  • High-stress, real-time environments
  • Variability across three residential sites

Results

By providing our stakeholders with a copy of our initial debrief toolkit and facilitator guide, we received immediate feedback that directly informed our overall deliverables. In the first iteration, the three program managers who viewed the resource immediately saw the document's applicability and its ability to be tailored to the needs of each IYR site. For the final deliverable, our two key stakeholders immediately indicated excitement for incorporating into IYR's program sites. Specifically, they both commented on the quality and comprehensiveness of the final product. As a starting point for IYR, the intentionality of the final deliverable will allow IYR leadership and users to adjust and modify it as needed once the kit is fully implemented.

Reflections

I chose this project because of my military background, where we routinely execute drills and conduct debriefs and after-action reports. Knowing the importance of lessons learned brought a personal interest to this request. Academically, this project reinforced that effective Instructional Design (ID) is not about delivering more information or always about training — it is about helping learners act in real-world environments. One of the most important shifts in my thinking was moving from a training-focused mindset to a systems-focused mindset: initially, I would have been prone to assume managers needed more instruction, but the analysis showed they needed better environmental support via a job aid. I also saw the value of addressing root causes rather than surface-level symptoms — by focusing on underlying factors influencing debrief quality, the solution supports a deeper level of organizational learning, often described as double-loop learning. This project also highlighted the importance of balancing structure with respect for existing expertise; many program managers and staff had valuable practice and experience, and what was needed was ID that targets debrief skills and enhances existing skillsets while building new ones. Finally, this experience strengthened my ability to make decisions alongside stakeholders — relying on feedback loops provided critical inputs that shaped the final product into a solution that met IYR's exact needs.
